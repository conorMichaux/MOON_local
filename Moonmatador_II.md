# Moonmatador_II

&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; Reached for his chest and groin at the same time. If there was a problem with
his heart, they'd have gone subclavian or venous. Maybe even arterial or transeptal. "The chest or the
groin," he thought. Nothing on his chest and nothing sensitive at either leg of his groin. A knock at the 
door and his mom was talking to him and making sounds but---he wasn't understanding. Still confused about
how untouched his body was, he blurted: "What did they do to my heart? I feel fine?" 

&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; His mother's words came into focus, slowly, over the course of the
conversation. His own words left his body to greet the words of hers, to digest the material of their
lives from outside of themselves. While the words did the lifting and the moving and the thinking for
him his mind drifted---"again with the drifting," he thought---towards fungi and how they digested things with enzymes
outside of their mycelial "bodies" before they "took them in." The language of "*took them in*" lingered for a
second. It was as if the fungi had to clean or prepare the food for entrance into the "home" of their
bodies. A fungal ritual, like having their guests take their shoes off at the door or washing up before a
meal. "Wait. That's all wrong. 'Took them in...'" He realized that "taking someone in" was not simply having a guest (and more than
that, wasn't breaking them down with enzymes). Francois reframed things from there. Started thinking
about food as a group or family of refugees and imagined that the breakage, the enzymatic digestion served as
dissolution of the "ego" or self or whatnot. At least in terms of the refugee comparison. It must be
humbling, maybe even humiliating to have to submit yourself to another person, another family. He thought
of "illegal" aliens, people living in foreign countries without the proper paperwork to be there. Part of
him felt a tugging metaphor at his sleeve, the idea that fungal systems digesting food from outside their
bodies wasn't unlike an alien spaceship abducting a human for experiments or the like. The digestive
enzymes serving as the tractor beam, a thing of motion and change. "So the two metaphors must be mirror images to each other,"
he speculated aloud. "The alien in a foreign land must submit; the alien in a foreign land must take."
Francois' eyes dilated a little. 

---What are you talking about, Francois?

---I'm just, becoming yarn again. Ignore me. I have questions about what happened; I'm just thinking, is
all.

---Okay, do you want some honey in your tea?

---Sure yes, please. 

&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; How does an extraterrestrial alien submit? The illegal migrant, they must
submit of course, and they must take no doubt. But why would an extraterrestrial submit? They clearly
take from us in those situations, take our biology and our data and our comfort. Unsettle us and scare
us, live in the margins of our psychology and sometimes take center stage. What about taking from us is a
form a submission? Francois' pupils were practically gone. The room was, accordingly, quite dark.

&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; His hand became warm, then hot. Honey and Earl Grey wafted up to him and
broke the constriction into something like a baseline diameter. 

---The hell was that, Franc?

---I'm not sure. I got tunnel vision and the... Maybe I should review Alice and Wonderland or something.
It felt like I went down the rabbithole. It even got dark in here for a minute.

---Do you want to know what happened with your heart?

---Yes. The last I remember was you getting in the car with me. I love you mom. Thank you for coming. I'd
have died.

---They didn't say much of what was wrong, to be honest with you. But they said you had something called
an FPA. Fingerprint Arrhythmia, is what it stands for.

---I thought it was sand in my heart.

---They said the sand caused it and that they just needed to get rid of the sand to fix you.

---OK... How? I don't have any access sites on my body...?
