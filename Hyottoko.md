# Hyottoko

&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; Dreaming. Otsuka woke from the drought at home, in his study. Felt as if he were lichen-encrusted stone.
In the past, droughts were nothing---blank spaces in his memory, with only his surroundings as evidence
for the passage of time. An arm shifted, a leg. He glanced behind himself before noticing the gentle
inspiration, expiration of his chest. He imagined the burning in his abdomen as whisky tearing at his 
stomach lining with machete as if it were invasive flora. The sun caught the edge of the claymore on his
wall. Then the pommel. How long had passed between edge of sword to pommel, he didn't know. But by this
point, the lichen encasing him had relented, or at least as far as his movements had asked of it. Images,
not unlike cotton candy dissolving in water slowly, lightly colored and sweetened his mind. There was
proprioception, and there was also perception, the understanding---and differentiation---between reality
and dream. The drought blankness, gone. Sensation of dreamless sleep was present, yet images still
greeted him like children excited to see a father after work.

&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; As the images dissolved, more and more, his heart sped up. For he began to
know their nature. Not of dream, of deja vu, or of reality. Whatever that word "reality" could
possibly mean anymore under the regime of the droughts. Squirming in his seat, his heart rate to began to
plateau. Began to turn his cheeks red, as if he were now some sort of animal triggering a defense
mechanism. If only people reacted to embarrassment the way predators did to prey.

&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; He wondered at that for a while. To avoid his shame. Meandered through the
thoughts of the animal kingdom's equivalents to shame. In some contexts, he thought, the red color would
trick the predator into thinking the potential food was poisonous or venomous. Almost like one of ours
wearing designer clothing or accessories, in an effort to be left alone or the opposite---depending on
who's asking. Turned his attention back to the human side of the equation: in some environments, like
that of the US, shame almost sort of attracted people whereas in others, repellent, abhorrent even.
Attracted people, yes. Like a sneeze. If you sneeze in a room full of strangers, no one will feel
obligated to you. Yet a group of people surrounding you, acquiantances will rush to hand you your
blessing. Grimace or sneer at your tears, but still try to comfort you, if a little nosily. Maybe shame
isn't so similar to predation, Otsuka thought. He dropped the subject with himself.
