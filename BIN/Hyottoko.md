# Hyottoko

&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; Dreaming. Otsuka woke from the drought at home, and
in his study. Felt as if he were lichen-encrusted stone. In the past, droughts
were nothing---blank spaces in his memory, with only his surroundings carrying
evidence for the passage of time. An arm shifted. Then a leg. He glanced behind
himself before noticing the gentle inspiration, expiration of his chest. He
imagined the burning in his abdomen as if whisky tearing at his stomach with
machete as if invasive flora. The sun caught the edge of the claymore on his
wall. Then the pommel. How long had passed between edge of sword to pommel, he
didn't know. But by this point, the lichen and his body had come to somewhat of
a compromise: he moving _this_ much---and it moving _that_ much. Images, not
unlike cotton candy dissolving in water, lightly colored and sweetened his mind.
There was proprioception, and there was also perception: the understanding---and
differentiation---between reality and dream. Differentiation between reality and
not was easy; he wondered at whatever "understanding" there could be between the
two. The drought blankness, gone. Sensation of dreamless sleep was present, yet
images still greeted him like children tugging at father fresh from work.

&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; As the images traveled and explored the streams of
his body, his heart sped up. And so he began to understand how his blood was,
because these images were not of dream, of deja vu, or of reality. Whatever that
word "reality" could possibly mean anymore under the regime of the droughts.
Squirming in his seat, heart rate beginning to plateau and with cheeks
candy-apple red, Otsuka knew. If only people reacted to embarrassment the way
predators did to prey.

&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; He wondered at that idea of defense mechanisms for a
while, to stall his shame and maybe thus defeat it that way. Meandered through
the thoughts of the animal kingdom's equivalents to shame. In some contexts, he
thought, the red color would trick the predator into thinking the prey was
poisonous or venomous. Almost like one of ours wearing designer clothing or
accessories, in an effort to be left alone or the opposite---depending on who's
asking. In some environments, like that of the US, shame could almost sort of
attract people whereas in others, it could be repellent, abhorrent even.
Attracted people, yes. Like a sneeze. If you sneeze in a room full of strangers,
no one will feel obligated to you. Yet a group of acquaintances surrounding you?
Easy way to discern who cares for you and who doesn't. Many will grimace or
sneer at your tears, yes, but some will still try to comfort you through their
own discomfort, if a little nosily. Maybe shame isn't so similar to predation,
Otsuka thought. He dropped the subject with himself.
