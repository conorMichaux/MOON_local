Drought

	Sya stood in her bathroom without a door and watched her eyes dilate in the mirror. It was
starting again. Finally. They called them droughts but she felt the opposite: the lack of drought
was drought. And here she was, on the bleeding edge of this new moment, a moment that would activate
her, turn her into something more. Something greater. Yet things didn't feel quite right. Part of
her felt a creeping tedium, a boredom with being "great." This feeling evaporated quickly, though. The
come-up, the dilation pleateaued then stabilized back into an appropriate radius. She wondered if this
process, this dilation of the pupil meant something deeper than just allowing more light into the
body.

	"Maybe," she thought, "maybe this dilation of the pupil is emblematic of making the student
(the pupil) greater." She wondered at this for a while, at the bodily process of allowing more light
into oneself. Considered the possibility that this phenomenon of the "drought" was influencing
people at a deeper level than just the eye. Influence at such a low level that we may not even
notice it, like a subtle but transient change in breathing or a twitch in the thumb that evades detection.

	"Odd," she mused. "The moon stops shining and what do our bodies do but let more light in. Our
bodies know. Mark my words." She laughed. No one was there to mark her words. The closest person would
have been her roommate, who was still asleep.

	This continued for some time. Sya, simply reveling in the influx of thoughts and feelings, outstretched a
fist, with her index finger pointing outward.

	The leap, the fall. The bridging of moments. She forgot what she intended to do and had to
reinvent a purpose for herself, because the act of index outstretch had already been committed.
Already written into the record.  

	With index ejected from fist, Sya did something odd. She holstered it back again. And then
drew it back out. Holstered. Back. Holstered. And this was how the moment was written, with playful
drawing and undrawing of herself. Of her own body.

	Then, with a jolt, she became aware of it. Sya felt as if helium were in her lungs. The
light that had entered her pupils a few moments before, in all its clinical awfulness, was
regressing her to a younger perspective. No, that wasn't right. Not even close. The light from
before was different, even if artificial and predictable. Synthetic. It had something peculiar about
it, sure, just as the dilation of her pupils had been inexplicable, too. 

	Something else was working on Sya. And she wondered if it was the same thing as was used on
the others. Or if it even was a "thing" to be used. Maybe it was a being, an entity. She didn't
know. But regardless, she felt a playfulness about her she hadn't felt in a long while. And it gave
her a sense of comfort. No brashness, no recklessness: comfort above all. She remembered old
childhood friends, from school and otherwise. Thought of their gestures and mannerisms and of the
symmetry of that, because she carried her own and saw that they could see hers, too. Sya. 

	Sya. She began to feel a deep peace. Catharsis? she wondered. A warm feeling spread across
her, spread through her mind. She felt an understanding of herself deepen and permeate her awareness. For
once, she looked back on her life, on those gestures and mannerisms of the people she'd known, and
realized that they all were operating on her in their own ways. Yes, for themselves and by themselves.
Alone and together, but they all exerted influence over her. In negative and positive ways. Like her dilated
pupils, she realized that these droughts in the sky weren't the first instance of subtle exertion and
influence on the world. These microscopic changes were always there, always altering and modifying and
mutating her. Along with everyone else, too.
