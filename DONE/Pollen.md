# Pollen

Seeded, it shook. Belled, it shook. Smiling, he shook his head. The
seeded gourd initiated, and the belled staff assented. And the old
shrink wondered how it was that they always knew before him.

Through all this rattling, the newest biographer sat firm in her rocking
chair.

They were on his ranch porch. He retired with stripes, she in search of
her own. But more than that, she wanted to make good on the symmetry of
her opportunity to work with him. He was not a local, regional, or
national hero, no. He saved humanity from the droughts. Solved them,
digested them before anyone else could. The renowned and experienced
biographers had been here already, she knew. It was the greenhorn's
chance now. The psychiatrist refused email and text. Preferred
face-to-face meetings, where nothing could be hidden of a person. Phone
calls were specifically and only used to coordinate or adjust plans.
More, less? Neither. All this may sound strict, but he knew it to be
necessary. There is a certain obligation when any experience must be
conveyed to another person. Let alone that this story will travel
through the chosen person's own lens and then through their pen, to
the rest of the world. What an aperture he was in search of. Good god,
he thought.

And yet there they sat. All these thoughts swirling and intermingling
and afloat in the neurochemistry. The surface tension was, accordingly,
too great. Great-big sky above, ever-so-slight ranch below: the ranch
may have been dry, but the sky? Milky blue.

His home verged on ocean-less bungalow in the Texas desert.
Water-strider? No no, that can't be right, more like a sky-strider, he
decided. The ranch was hardly what she had imagined. In much politer
packaging, she made comment on this. Hoping to break the tension
herself. It didn't. Maybe not polite enough, she thought.

The doctor's cane rattled as the breeze rustled. For a moment she wasn't
sure if the two, the cane and the breeze, were linked somehow. As if one
were causing the other. Feeling a rattle of her own and thinking maybe
he'll appreciate some boldness, she asked what everyone was thinking:

---How did you solve the droughts, doctor?

---To answer that question. Would be to write the book.

---You mean your whole life...

---The events of my, built me into the person who could protect...

---Us.

---Yes. Anyone in my position could have done it.

---I don't know that everyone would, though.

---I avoid speculating like that. For my own sake.

The doctor punctuated this last with a lifting of his rattle cane and a
releasing of it. The biographer, who was trying to quit smoking, snapped
off a nib of her candy cigarette. The shrink loosed a light, dry laugh
of appreciation. Her mind wandered back to just a few moments before;
the handshake between rattle and breeze struck her a second time. With a
small tilt of the head and a smile, she returned her attention to him:

---Well why don't we start toward the beginning, what is an early memory
or event that primed you for solving the droughts?

---Ah.

---...

The psychiatrist seemed to rattle his cane without moving it.

---What's wrong, doctor?

---I wouldn't say there is anything wrong in how you've phrased your
question, but I do think it should be said that it is an arboreal one.

---How do you mean?

---It seeks to build a foundation first, seeks to start from the bottom
and move upwards toward the present layer or canopy of the moment. What
I would like for you to internalize for yourself, not for the biography
I want you to write of me, is that life doesn't always have to be that
way.

---I see. What are some examples? I'm curious, sir.

---Ah well, that gets into writing the whole book again. Part of the solution to the
droughts was realizing this rhizomatic structure within the world. Look at me, for
example. I solved them within my hospital system and the solution spread from that
site in a quite fragmented manner.

---Can we define rhizome, please?

Catherine brought out her voice recorder. Dr. Otsuka asked for a candy
cigarette. After immediately snapping off a nib of his own as if
preparing a fountain pen, he began again.

---Rhizome in this case refers to a group of elements. In this case all life on the
planet. None of these elements are linked, at least not by proximity, not even
necessarily by relationship or knowledge of each other.

---Is it off-base to use the language of quantum mechanics here, doctor?

---You might develop that understanding from someone more fluent in it,
but yes it seems a useful framework, at least from where I sit. Yet it's
important to realize that quantum entanglement and the other terms do
not capture the droughts. Not in their entirety.

---I see, but please continue. Let's get back to the rhizome.

---Yes. I'm fond of the neighborhood metaphor. Which is to say, we all belong to a
vast neighborhood, Jung called it the collective unconscious. I call it a
neighborhood. Call me Mr. Rogers if you like.

---I will not but thank you for the offer.

---Yes well the offer's shelf life is about a week. I like the idea of
him having tattoo sleeves under those cardigans. But anyway,

---Is that why you're wearing a puffer jacket, doctor?

---Don't slander me on your recording!

The doctor leaned toward the recorder, enunciating each word,

---Please, Catherine the Editor. Don't let them know I'm running cold
these days! Resist Catherine the Biographer!

For the first time in quite a while, Catherine loosed her own light laugh.
Although her tally of "light laughs" wasn't all that well-kept, it still struck her.

---B'yes, to answer your question, the neighborhood metaphor describes
us as belonging to a collective. Much of this has been explained by Jung
already. The archetypes and so on, but the twist here is those droughts.
Usually these connections and communications only occur subconsciously
or during sleep. During the droughts, though? ... Pandora's Box? *Open
season.*

Catherine and Dr. Otsuka were both rocking in their chairs by this
point. Eagerly snapping away at candy cigarettes (Catherine brought a
box, not a pack or carton) and shooting the breeze together. At some
point, Dr. Otsuka broke the flow of conversation about strategy in All
5s Dominos and said:

---Catherine.

---Eh uh yes? I didn't mean to say that snake eyes is a bad domino! I
just meant that it's loaded with bad luck for me...

---Catherine, we've discussed dominos and pizza. Baseball, beer, and
music. Literature. Do you know what this represents, what this means.

She saw his point immediately. She'd taken classes and done workshops.
Understood, abstractly, how biographies written and, more broadly, how
journalism was conducted. But until Dr. Otsuka posed his question, she
hadn't felt the purpose of the theory.

---Doctor. A lot just clicked into place for me. I'm new to this and
well I mean I'm just out of college recently...

---Catherine, I need for you to show that you understand.

---A good biography requires a rhizome of a person. Not an arboreal
model. The bits and pieces that inform the whole of itself.

Yes.
