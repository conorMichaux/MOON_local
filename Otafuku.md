# Otafuku

&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; The ports on her body always gone by the time she woke
up. For a time, she cycled between the theory of being hurt by others and of hurting
herself---and didn't see either option as very plausible. Despite that, anything was
plausible under the auspices of the droughts. With time and repetition, she finally
came to peace with the droughts and she found herself at the base of the mountain:
she was using the droughts to heal others.

&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; It made sense from where she was sitting. During the
droughts, patients would present to her as having the symptoms of her day job
patients: heart problems. Namely, tachy and bradyarhythmias. Mostly tachy, which is
to say fast heart rates caused by _something_ within the heart of the patient. The
solution, in waking life at least, was to identify the mechanism or mechanisms and
address them by selectively damaging the tissue to halt whatever circuit within the
heart there was. This all was, as she learned, very different during a drought.
Instead of sedating the patient or damaging _the patient's_ heart, Dr. Otafuku would
herself get on the patient table in the cath lab for the procedure and allow the
patient in question to speak freely and from the heart. More often than not, Dr.
Otafuku would not listen to the content of the patient's speech. Rather she would pay
close attention to something deeper, something inexpressible in the way they spoke.
Her team at the hospital would gain access via her groin if it was the first case for
the drought and conduct the procedure somewhat normally as compared to the day-to-day
version, but this was all done with the patient there, in the room, talking and
sometimes crying. They would watch Dr. Otafuku grimace and murmur and whelp in pain.
It was an upsetting thing to see someone go through for you. Some of the patients
didn't live the most healthy lifestyles, and so for them, this process was harrowing;
it opened their eyes in a way that the day-to-day version of the procedures couldn't.
In fact, the normal procedures typically _closed_ their eyes, caused them to forget
and generally fumble through the haze of the healing process. Our Dr. Otafuku,
though, was somewhat of a specialist. As in, no-one-else-could-do-this type of
specialist. Other cardiac electrophysiologists had their own gifts or tricks or what
have you, but none quite met the symbolic level that Dr. Otafuku's reached. Because
the procedure was done in sterile fashion, even the patient was there at the table in
a surgical gown, mask, and bouffant or cap. The patients who preferred to use
radiation for the procedure would be wearing heavy lead, that was weighted
dynamically: the more they felt, the less the lead weighed; the less they felt... The
drought tended to get through to them, let's just say. 

&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; What didn't initially make sense to Dr. Otafuku was
this process of hearing without listening. As soon as she put language to it
though, it immediately became clear that the process she was facilitating, along
with her patient, stood in direct opposition to much of the training she had
received in her life, specifically regarding bedside manner and collecting
patient history. How could you hear without listening? This question tore her up
on the backend of the early droughts, it undermined a lot of what she believed in.
Particularly because the traditional skill set in question was something she had to
work at a little longer than her peers had. It took time for her to pick up on subtle
cues and clues. Frequently she would reduce her earliest patients in her career down
to what was on paper, not out of spite or anything so malevolent, but simply because
their narrative was shaky, clumsy, and usually error-prone. Patients lied and
generally weren't fluent in her heart language, but tests didn't lie and tests were
the language itself, for her anyway. Yet there she was in just this last drought,
listening to a patient drone on and on about the silliest of things. Or so she was
told, because of course _she_ wasn't listening. Her staff didn't run on fumes;
rather, they ran on sighs and good-natured, time-killing humor. They generally got
antsy throughout these cases, though, because they just didn't have "it": they had no
idea when the patient was close to having the arrhythmia in question solved and
sorted. And so, apart from the mechanical work of assisting the patient with the
procedure and access and getting catheters up into Dr. Otafuku's heart, the staff had
nothing to do and it all kind of just felt like a big fancy waiting room to them.
And, honestly, it wasn't such a bad thing. Boring yes, but also good fun. For when
the staff forgot themselves and joined in the motion of the drought, they quite
enjoyed the procedures that Dr. Otafuku ran.

&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; The best days were when the patient communed with
Otafuku and the decision was made to call in the ultrasound retinue. Much
excitement filled the air when this came about because the ultrasound team was not
man and machine the way it was during the waking hours. Instead, ultrasound entailed
two species: human and bat. Usually megabats, but sometimes micro if a patient (and
so therefore Otafuku herself) was of small stature. Bats worked with other
specialties too, of course, but the ultrasound techs had the most charisma and fun.
Anesthesia, for example, had a close bond with vampire bats (as one might expect).
