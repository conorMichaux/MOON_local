# Elephant

&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; A fellow patient and friend to Caroline, Saya: she bit a
bit harder, felt things a bit more fiercely. It's easy to frame her this way. Harder
is framing her the way she prefers. Namely, as a patron of the moon. This difficulty
not on the basis of writing, but because it requires a certain amount of explanation
and hand waving and whatnot. 

&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; A most important factor to Saya's relationship with the
moon was primarily temporal: she considered herself a patron _before_ the droughts
began. Part of her wondered if she triggered them or facilitated their need. Maybe
the moon was intervening to help her specifically? Who knows. Regardless, Saya was
sober now. The droughts eclipsed any and all need for drug. She experimented with
food and water too. Still needed those, sadly. 

&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; She felt like a cartoon character under the moon's
protection or, rather, within the "droughts" as the layfolk liked to call them. She
preferred the Janus Moon idea. Not flower, not blue, not blood, not super, not full,
not new. Janus. The Roman God of liminality, of crossing, of transition. He was the
Papa Legba of the Roman people. Janus, his name associated with closing and opening.
Janus, his face doubled. His face pointing to the past and his face pointing towards
the future. A friend of Saya's, just before a new drought---and perhaps _causing_ a
new one---had been carving wood and plunged the knife into his wrist. She'd been the
first, he'd said, to ask or suggest or entertain the question of intent. EMS hadn't,
the NP hadn't, his family hadn't. Saya addressed it and allowed him to confirm the
accident. No further questioning and, most importantly, earnest belief in his story.
She simply wanted to offer the question out of care. She'd want the same from a
friend, too.

&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; Unaffected palm lines but his palm is numb now, along
with his wedding ring finger.
